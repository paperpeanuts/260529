THE BIG LIE

Projections, Distortions, and the Cartographic Deception That Shapes Military Decision-Making

1.1 — THE SHAPE OF TRUTH

Every military operation begins with a deceptively simple question: where is it? The answer depends on what you think "where" means — and more critically, what shape you believe the ground beneath you to be.

Perfect sphere — assumed by early navigators. Error: up to 21km at the poles.

The Earth is not a sphere. It is not even an ellipsoid. It is a geoid — an irregular, lumpy mass distorted by gravitational anomalies, mantle convection, and mountain ranges. The difference between a perfect ellipsoid and the geoid ranges from -106m to +85m.

The WGS84 geoid model used by GPS systems has an accuracy of plus or minus 2m globally. In contested environments where GPS is denied, legacy astro-geodetic methods revert to local datums with errors exceeding 50m.

For a ballistic missile with a CEP of 100m, a 50m datum error consumes half the error budget before the missile even leaves the rail.

1.2 — FLATTENING THE IMPOSSIBLE

You cannot flatten a sphere without distortion. This is not a limitation of technology — it is a mathematical impossibility proven by Gauss's Theorema Egregium in 1827.

Mercator (1569) preserves angles — making it ideal for maritime navigation. A bearing drawn on a Mercator map is a true bearing on the ocean. But the cost is catastrophic area distortion that increases exponentially toward the poles.

During the Falklands War (1982), British forces initially used Mercator charts for amphibious planning, resulting in range estimates that were off by 18 percent — corrected only after a Royal Navy surveyor identified the projection mismatch.

1.3 — THE BIG LIE

In 2005, Google Maps adopted Web Mercator (EPSG:3857) — a variant that cuts off at plus or minus 85.05 degrees latitude and uses a spherical formula rather than the true ellipsoidal one. It is technically not even a proper map projection. It is a hack.

Web Mercator preserves neither area nor distance. At 60N, areas are inflated by 400%. At 70N — where Greenland sits — by 950%. Greenland appears the same size as Africa on your screen. Africa is 14 times larger.
