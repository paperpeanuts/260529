1.1 — Earth Is Not a Sphere

Standard military education teaches Earth as an oblate spheroid — an ellipse rotated about its polar axis. The equatorial radius (6,378.137 km) exceeds the polar radius (6,356.752 km) by roughly 21 kilometres.

But the real surface — the geoid — is far more irregular. Gravitational anomalies from mantle density variations, ocean trenches, mountain roots, and subsurface structures create a surface that undulates by over 100 metres from the reference ellipsoid.

In GIS we call it informally "the potato." In geodesy, it is the equipotential surface of Earth's gravity field that best fits mean sea level. Every coordinate you have ever used sits on an approximation of this shape.

1.2 — The Cartographer's Bargain

BRIEFING NOTE — ORIGIN OF THE PROBLEM

You cannot flatten a sphere without distortion. This is not a limitation of technology — it is a theorem. Gauss's Theorema Egregium (1827) proved that a surface's curvature is an intrinsic property: no flat map can preserve all of area, shape, distance, and direction simultaneously.

Every map projection is a deliberate choice about which truth to sacrifice. Mercator (1569) sacrifices area to preserve angles — invaluable for maritime navigation, where a constant bearing appears as a straight line. But the cost is catastrophic at high latitudes.

Web Mercator (EPSG:3857) — the projection underlying virtually every web map since Google Maps in 2005 — compounds this. It is not even a true conformal projection: it uses a spherical formula for an ellipsoidal Earth, introducing additional systematic error that the specification itself acknowledges: "not to be used for authoritative geodetic purposes."

1.4 — Tolerance for Error

HISTORICAL CONTEXT — WORLD WAR II

During Operation Overlord (1944), artillery survey teams worked with local datums derived from triangulation networks established decades earlier. A 50-metre position error on a paper map translated directly to a 50-metre miss on the ground. For area bombardment against a dispersed target, this was acceptable. For close-support missions near friendly lines, it was lethal. The maps themselves used local projections — the British Cassini Grid, the French Lambert system — each introducing their own distortions that varied by region.

MODERN CONTEXT — PRECISION STRIKE

In 1991, Tomahawk cruise missiles required GPS-derived coordinates mapped to WGS 84, then transformed to the local datum of the target region. The geodetic transformation — the mathematical bridge between two reference frames — had to be accurate to metres. A 1-metre error in the geodetic model could mean the difference between a command bunker destroyed and a civilian shelter hit. Modern JDAMs advertise circular error probable (CEP) of less than 5 metres. The projection and datum are no longer background noise — they are part of the weapon system.

THE FUNDAMENTAL CONSTANT

The geodesy has not changed. The ellipsoid models, the gravity field measurements, the coordinate transformations — the mathematics is the same mathematics used at the International Bureau of Weights and Measures in 1924. What changed is the tolerance for error. When your weapon flies itself to within 3 metres of a coordinate, every metre of projection distortion is a metre of risk.
